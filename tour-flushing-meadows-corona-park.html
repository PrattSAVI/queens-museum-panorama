Tour Flushing Meadows Corona Park

Welcome to Flushing Meadows Corona Park!

In Flushing Meadows Corona Park, numerous structures remain from the 1964-1965 New York World's Fair including: the Unisphere, the New York State Pavilion, the Port Authority Heliport (now the Terrace on the Park restaurant), the New York Hall of Science, and the New York City Building (now the Queens Museum). The New York State Marine Amphitheater, demolished in 1996, remains on the map at the edge of Meadow Lake. The Queens Zoo, opposite the Queens Museum over the Grand Central Parkway, was erected in 1968 to occupy the central lawns of the former Transportation Zone. Citi Field and the USTA Billie Jean King National Tennis Center are also depicted nearby the Mets-Willets Point MTA subway station.

The Unisphere

The Unisphere, the symbol of the 1964–1965 New York World's Fair's theme "Peace Through Understanding".

The New York State Pavilion

The New York State Pavilion hosted daily events and performances on the ground floor beneath the "Tent of Tomorrow," with two cafeterias situated within the spherical platforms.

Queens Theatre

Theaterama, now known as the Queens Theatre, was also a part of the New York State Pavilion. Architect Philip Johnson designed the entire complex, and also commissioned new artworks to be installed on the facade for the World's Fair.

Queens Museum

The Queens Museum, formerly known as the New York City Building, is the only relic remaining from the 1939–1940 New York World's Fair.

The New York Hall of Science

The New York Hall of Science, originally called The New York City Museum of Science and Technology, was created for the 1964–1965 World's Fair. It was accompanied by a "Space Park," with various spacecrafts and rockets borrowed from NASA.

Heliport/Queens Zoo

The Triborough Bridge and Tunnel Authority's Heliport, built as a literal landing pad and restaurant, is now known as the Terrace on the Park. Sandwiched between the Grand Central Parkway and that building is now the Queens Zoo. This plot of land was formerly occupied by six pavilions at the 1964–1965 World's Fair.

Citi Field

What we now know as Citi Field, the home of the Mets baseball team, was formerly Shea Stadium. Shea Stadium opened just days before the 1964–1965 World's Fair.

Amphitheater

The Roman-styled Ampitheater was originally the site of Billy Rose's Aquacade at the 1939–1940 New York World's Fair, then housed the Florida Pavilion's "Waterski Extravagaza" at 1964–1965 World's Fair. It was demolished in 1996 due to neglect.
